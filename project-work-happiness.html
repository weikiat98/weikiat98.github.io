Does Working Harder
Make You Happier?

In South Korea, they call it "gwarosa". In Japan, "karoshi" — death by overwork. But what if 7 years of data across 45 countries tells a different story?

Introduction

The Work-Happiness Paradox

Long working hours are negatively associated with stress, depression, and low subjective well-being. However, not every economy shares the same narrative. Some economies achieve better happiness results despite working longer hours.

Could better working conditions justify working longer? Could an economy with higher GDP per capita provide a more advantageous starting point for happiness? is happiness dependent on the region economies are located due to differences in working culture? We investigate this complex relationship between work and happiness using data from OECD, World Bank, and the World Happiness Report.

The relationship between work and happiness is not significant to a large extent. Better working conditions do not necessarily justify workers working longer.

Finding 01

Wealthier Nations Tend to Be Happier

People living in economies with higher GDP per capita tend to be happier and have a more positive outlook in life. The data reveals a clear correlation between economic prosperity and life satisfaction scores.

Key Insight

Luxembourg consistently ranks highest in both GDP per capita and life satisfaction, demonstrating the strong positive correlation between economic prosperity and happiness.

The scatter plot analysis shows that countries in the excellent GDP category (above $50,000) consistently report life ladder scores above 7.0, while countries in the poor GDP category (below $20,000) average around 5.1.

Finding 02

Long Working Hours Unhappiness

Contrary to popular belief, not all hardworking countries are less happy. The data challenges the assumption that long working hours automatically lead to lower life satisfaction and increased negative emotions.

Singapore works 45+ hours per week yet maintains high positive emotions — challenging the narrative that associates long hours with depression and low well-being.

The Singapore Paradox

Despite averaging 45+ working hours per week, Singapore maintains a positive affect score of approximately 0.68 — higher than many European countries with shorter work weeks. This suggests that factors beyond working hours influence happiness.

The analysis of working hours against happiness metrics reveals that regional and cultural factors play a significant role. Asian economies like Singapore demonstrate that long working hours can coexist with relatively high positive emotions.

Finding 03

Working Conditions Matter More Than Hours

While working hours alone don't determine happiness, working conditions play a crucial role. Factors like paid annual leave, severance pay, and employee protections contribute significantly to life satisfaction.

Paid Leave Analysis

Countries with generous paid annual leave (30+ days) like Finland and France consistently show higher life ladder scores, while the US with no mandated paid leave presents an interesting exception.

Regional Analysis

Culture Shapes the Work-Happiness Relationship

Happiness is dependent on region due to differences in working culture, making it a subjective topic. Europe and Scandinavian regions can have lower working hours while Latin America and Asia regions work longer — yet each finds its own path to well-being.

Europe & Scandinavia

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

Wealth Enables Happiness

Countries with higher GDP per capita consistently show higher life satisfaction, positive emotions, and lower negative emotions.

Hours Happiness

Long working hours don't automatically lead to unhappiness. Cultural context and economic opportunity matter more.

Conditions Count

Working conditions like paid leave improve life satisfaction, but cultural factors can compensate for their absence.

The relationship between work and happiness is not significant to a large extent. Better working conditions do not necessarily justify workers working longer, as shown from results in Europe and Scandinavian regions.

People living in poorer economic environments are less likely to pursue their passions and more inclined to work longer to make ends meet. In comparison, people in wealthier societies have more opportunities to pursue hobbies or passions that make them happy.

Ultimately, happiness is a subjective and culturally-dependent concept. The data highlights the limitations of improved working conditions and emphasizes the complex nature of happiness and well-being.
